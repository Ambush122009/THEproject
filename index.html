A Museum of Wealth in the 2020s

ARTIFACT 01

The Anime Gold Rush

The anime industry BOOMED in the 2020s, due to the boredom that came with home arrest during covid, transforming from niche community into a multi-billion dollar global economy. Demon Slayer: Mugen Train became the highest-grossing film of 2020 worldwide, earning over $500 million. Streaming platforms like Crunchyroll sold to Sony for $1.2 billion in 2021, while anime conventions became massive gatherings fueling local passon and economy.

Global anime market: $30+ billion by 2025

This artifact represents how cultural products once dismissed as "cartoons" became legitimate wealth generators, creating millionaire voice actors, animators, and content creators. Wealth in the 2020s isn't just in traditional industries like business or engineering but also in storytelling that brought people all over the world together in an era where no one could even step outside.

ARTIFACT 02

AI: Creating and Destroying Fortunes

ChatGPT reached 100 million users faster than any app in history. OpenAI's valuation boalstered to $157 billion by 2025, while AI art tools like Midjourney created new creative economies. But this wealth came with distruction too: voice actors, artists, and writers watched AI tools trained on their work threaten the things they worked their entire lives for.

New roles created: Prompt engineers, AI ethicists, AI trainers
Jobs threatened: Traditional artists, writers, customer service

Wealth in the AI age goes to people who own the technology, not really to the whose labor Ccreated it in the first palce. This artifact shows how innovation creates billionaires while simultaneously disrupting millions of lower and middle class workers which happens multiple times through the American economic history.

ARTIFACT 03

Digital Gold and Fool's Gold

Bitcoin hit an all-time high of $69,000 in 2021, literally creating overnight millionaires and reviving the American dream of sudden wealth. NFTs sold for millions, Beeple's digital art earned $69 million. But by 2022, the crypto market crashed, destroying $2 trillion in value.

Peak crypto market cap: $3 trillion (2021)
Lives ruined by speculation and scams

Gatsby's parties funded by mysterious business dealings, likewise crypto promised wealth without clear sources. It represented the corruption of the get-rich-quick schemes that leave ordinary Americans holding the gambeling their bag while early adopters cashed out on the oppurtunity.

ARTIFACT 04

Billionaires Play Astronaut

Jeff Bezos, Elon Musk, and Richard Branson all spent billions racing to space while their employees struggled with warehouse conditions and union-rights. Bezos's 11-minute space flight cost roughly $5.5 billion in development—money that could have given each of his 1.3 million employees a $4,000 bonus.

Cost of billionaire space race: $10+ billion
U.S. child poverty rate: 16.9% in 2024

This artifact embodies the horrifing wealth inequality of the 2020s. Like Tom and Daisy Buchanan's careless wealth in Gatsby, these wasteful projects represent how extreme wealth disconnects people from reality. The 2020s showed us that money doesn't buy happiness: it buys rockets to escape the problems that the same money created.

ARTIFACT 05

Selling Dreams with every post

The influencer economy exploded to $21 billion by 2023, with top creators earning millions from sponsored posts. MrBeast became the highest-paid YouTuber, earning $54 million in 2021 alone. TikTok created a new class of teenage millionaires who monetized their lives for our entertainment.

Top influencers earn $20M+ annually
99% of aspiring influencers earn less than minimum wage

Like Gatsby's green light, the influencer lifestyle represents the unattainable dream for millions. Kids chase viral fame while algorithms exploit their content for their own selfish profit. This artifact shows how wealth in the 2020s is performative. You only as rich as your last posts shows.

ARTIFACT 06

The $1.7 Trillion chains

Student loan debt reached $1.7 trillion by 2024, shackling an entire generation. College costs increased 180% since 1980 while wages stayed practically the same. The promise that education leads to wealth became a cruel joke. The same education that promises us jobs and houses right out of college now gives us layers of debt and a more selective job market then ever before.

Average debt per borrower: $38,000
Average repayment time: 20 years

This artifact reveals how wealth in the 2020s is more and more gatekept. The American Dream now needs one to take crushing debt for a chance, not even a garuntee, at stability. Unlike Gatsby's self-made (little illegal) fortune, the next generation of the workforce must choose between education and financial freedom.

ARTIFACT 07

The Great Divide

By 2024, the top 1% of Americans held 32% of the nation's wealth, while the bottom 50% held just 2%. Billionaire wealth increased by 70% during the pandemic while 60% of Americans lived paycheck to paycheck. The gap between rich and poor reached levels that were not seen since the 1920s. Which was right before the Great Depression.

CEO-to-worker pay ratio: 399:1 (2024)
Middle class shrinking annually since 1971

This final artifact answers the most essential question most directly: wealth in the 2020s is not a way to happiness but a corrupting force that divides America in to shards. Like the moral emptiness of the Buchanans' mansion, extreme wealth exists right next door to poverty, blind to the suffering it causes. The 2020s taught us that money can't buy meaning, community, or equity...but it can buy rockets, NFTs, and temporary distraction from these truths.

Gatsby's Question, Our Answer

The 2020s answered Fitzgerald's century-old question about wealth with clarity. Money didn't bring happiness. it brought rockets for billionaires and debt for students. It created AI fortunes while destroying the regular workers. It turned culture into commodity, whether through anime streaming wars or influencer marketing. The green light Gatsby chased has now become our phone screens, promising wealth and fulfillment while delivering only pain and inequality.
"So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past."
— F. Scott Fitzgerald, The Great Gatsby
A hundred years after Gatsby, we're still chasing the same illusion: that wealth equals worth, that money can fill the void in our hearts, that the next big thing will finally deliver the American Dream. The 2020s showed us that technology changes, but human nature and the corruption of extreme wealth remains constant.
